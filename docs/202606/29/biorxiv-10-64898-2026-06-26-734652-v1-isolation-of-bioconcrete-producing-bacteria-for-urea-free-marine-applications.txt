Abstract 25 

26 

Manual intervention for concrete repair and replacement comes at high environmental and 27 

economic costs. Bioconcrete, which can be formed by bacteria via microbially-induced 28 

carbonate precipitation (MICP), is a sustainable method for concrete repair. Bioconcrete-forming 29 

bacteria can be incorporated into the concrete at mixing and then heal cracks where and when 30 

they occur. Bioconcrete is not intentionally made by bacteria; rather, it is a byproduct of 31 

alterations to the local environment that occur during their normal metabolic activities. Bacteria 32 

thus “make” bioconcrete by different metabolic mechanisms, and the environment plays a 33 

substantial role in the yield and physical properties of the bioconcrete produced by a given 34 

bacterium. The ureolytic bacterium Sporosarcina pasteurii is the most commonly used model 35 

organism for MICP, but it requires urea supplementation, which is not feasible for all applications 36 

because of nitrogenous waste. In particular, the marine environment is understudied for 37 

bioconcrete applications, yet there is a need for self-healing structures in this environment, 38 

wherein urea and nitrogenous waste would be detrimental to native biota. Here, we assessed 39 

the ability of S. pasteurii to form bioconcrete under marine-like media conditions with urea and 40 

calcium supplementation. We found that S. pasteurii generated higher bioconcrete yields in 41 

these media conditions compared to standard growth media. We then designed an enrichment 42 

protocol to isolate and characterize non-urea-requiring bioconcrete-forming bacteria from 43 

Atlantic seawater. We identified three isolates, from the Sulflitobacter , Marinobacter , and 44 

Bacillus genera, two of which yielded higher bioconcrete yields in seawater-mimicking media 45 

compared to non-ureolytic bacteria utilized in prior literature. Moreover, scanning electron 46 

microscopy (SEM)/energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) and Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) 47 

spectroscopy revealed distinct chemical and structural features of the bioconcrete produced by 48 

bacteria in seawater-mimicking medium and between ureolytic and non-ureolytic cultures. 49 

Overall, our work establishes a pipeline for the isolation and characterization of novel 50 

bioconcrete-forming bacteria from marine samples, with potential for application to marine self-51 

healing materials. 52

Introduction 55 

With the need to reduce global CO 2 emissions, bioconcrete is increasingly researched 56 

as a sustainable alternative to manual concrete repair and replacement arising due to structure 57 

degradation via cracking. Bioconcrete, also commonly called microbially induced carbonate 58 

precipitation (MICP), is a byproduct of normal bacterial metabolic activities that produce suitable 59 

environmental conditions to precipitate calcium carbonate (1). Several bacterial metabolic 60 

activities contribute to creating optimum conditions for bioconcrete formation. Among the most 61 

studied are urease activity, carbonic anhydrase activity, and denitrification (1–3). A specific 62 

bacterium may use one or more of these as part of its normal metabolic activities. These 63 

mechanisms result in changes in the local environment around the bacterial cell that increase 64 

the local pH, calcium, and carbonate concentration, thus allowing the bacterial cell surface to 65 

act as a nucleation site for calcium carbonate, or bioconcrete, precipitation (1,4). 66 

Urease activity is the most extensively studied mechanism for bioconcrete production, 67 

owing to its rapid and plentiful production of bioconcrete. The ureolytic mechanism of MICP 68 

starts with urea degradation by the enzyme urease, producing ammonia and carbon dioxide. 69 

Ammonia raises the local pH, promoting the formation of bicarbonate from carbon dioxide. The 70 

calcium from a calcium precursor, such as calcium acetate or calcium chloride, is attracted to 71 

the negatively charged cell surface. Together, these increase the local concentrations of calcium 72 

and bicarbonate around the cell’s surface, allowing the cell surface to act as a nucleation site for 73 

the precipitation of calcium carbonate (5). 74 

Similar to urease activity, the carbonic anhydrase mechanism for bioconcrete production 75 

relies on an alkaline pH to precipitate calcium carbonate. Carbonic anhydrase is a widespread 76 

enzyme, with all three major classes found in prokaryotes. Carbonic anhydrases reversibly 77 

catalyze the conversion of CO 2 to HCO 3-, both of which can be used by the cell for metabolic or 78 

transport purposes (6). As a byproduct of these activities, bicarbonate can accumulate or be 79 

transported outside of the cell where the presence of calcium allows the precipitation of calcium 80 

carbonate. 81 

Apart from these mechanisms, several other factors impact bioconcrete production, such 82 

as the choice of microbe and that microbe’s specific metabolic abilities, availability of 83 

nutrients/minerals, culture or growth conditions (such as temperature and oxygenation), quantity 84 

of bacteria used, and bacterial metabolic state (vegetative vs dormant/endospore) (3,7). The 85 

culture medium provides nutrients and energy sources, influencing bacterial growth. In addition, 86

calcium carbonate produced by the bacteria (8). With sufficient levels of the appropriate 88 

nutrients, bacterial cells can actively (vegetatively) replicate. In contrast, bacterial endospores 89 

are dormant, metabolically inactive cells that can withstand extreme environmental pressures 90 

such as high heat, pressure, and desiccation that usually kill vegetative bacteria (9). The 91 

hardiness of bacterial endospores allows them to survive their incorporation into concrete during 92 

mixing and pouring. Also, the ability to remain dormant in the concrete for long periods of time 93 

ensures the bacteria remain viable when the concrete later cracks (10). Once the concrete 94 

cracks, there is an ingress of water and nutrients that allows the endospores within the crack to 95 

germinate back into a vegetative state. The resumption of normal metabolic activity facilitates 96 

bioconcrete formation and aids in the healing of cracks (11). The Gram-positive endospore-97 

forming bacterium Sporosarcina pasteurii is the most commonly used model organism for 98 

bioconcrete production due to its high urease activity, which improves its bioconcrete yield, and 99 

its endospore-forming ability, which allows its incorporation into concrete mixtures (4). 100 

The marine environment is not a commonly researched application environment for 101 

bioconcrete, although there is a need for sustainable repair of marine structures (12). The 102 

marine environment presents several challenges that do not apply to terrestrial bioconcrete. 103 

One is that the high salinity of the environment can greatly impact bacterial growth (13), 104 

especially for soil microorganisms like S. pasteurii (14). Another is that urea, and the ammonium 105 

resulting from urease activity, may be detrimental to marine life. This is particularly relevant for 106 

the construction of self-healing artificial coral reef structures or other structures to support 107 

marine recolonization or food production (for e.g., oyster beds). The use of bioconcrete by non-108 

urease producing organisms may make underwater concrete structures more sustainable in the 109 

long term due to self-healing capabilities, reducing the need for human intervention for repair or 110 

replacement. 111 

Since bioconcrete is a byproduct of normal metabolic activity, bacteria must actively use 112 

these pathways for its production. And, because different bacteria may respond differently to the 113 

same environment (for our study, the marine environment), it is important to test and compare 114 

different strains to identify the most suitable candidate for a specific environment or application. 115 

Here, we tested the ability of S. pasteurii to form bioconcrete under culture conditions mimicking 116 

non-marine and marine settings, all of which required the addition of urea. We then designed 117 

and implemented an enrichment culture method to isolate novel, non-urea-dependent 118 

bioconcrete-forming bacteria from seawater collected from the Atlantic coast of the United 119

Ahb. pseudofirmus and the sea isolates at day 16) using a protocol modified from Reeksting et 192

Overnight cultures were grown in the routine medium specified in Table 1 for the specific 195 

bacterium. The optical density of the culture was measured at 600 nm (OD 600 ) using a Thermo 196 

Scientific GENESYS 30 Visible Spectrophotometer. These readings were used to calculate the 197 

volume of the overnight culture required to inoculate the bioconcrete quantification medium to a 198 

starting OD 600 of 0.001. For S. pasteurii , 50 mL of bioconcrete quantification medium was 199 

inoculated with the calculated amount of the overnight culture and incubated. After 2 hours, 15 200 

mL culture was aliquoted into 50 mL centrifuge tubes. Since the other bacterial species in this 201 

study were analyzed at only one timepoint, this step was omitted for them. Instead, 15 mL of 202 

quantification medium was inoculated directly in a 50 mL centrifuge tube. For all bacteria, the 203 

lids of the 50 mL tubes were taped in place one half turn from being completely shut to allow 204 

consistent oxygenation throughout the experiment. The tubes were incubated until their 205 

timepoint for quantification. A tube was removed for quantification at the timepoint 1, 6, and/or 206 

16 days after inoculation and vortexed thoroughly. The pH was measured by dripping 30 μL of 207 

culture onto pH strips (Fisher Brand). Serial dilutions were performed (1:10 dilutions in sterile 208 

phosphate buffered saline) to calculate the viable cell count, or colony forming units (CFU)/mL, 209 

remaining in the culture. These dilutions were plated using the track dilution method described 210 

by Jett et al. (24). The colonies were typically counted after 3 days to calculate CFU/mL in the 211 

bioconcrete culture. The rest of the culture, still in the 50 mL centrifuge tube, was then 212 

centrifuged at 980 x g for 4 minutes (Sorvall RC 6+ Centrifuge with F13S-14x50cy rotor), and 213 

the supernatant was aspirated. The pellet was washed with 40 mL of autoclaved deionized 214 

water, resuspended by vortexing the pellet, recentrifuged, and the supernatant was aspirated. 215 

The pellet was washed in this manner a total of three times. After washing, the pellet was 216

Robust production of compositionally distinct bioconcrete by S. pasteurii in 258 

media mimicking seawater and reef, supplemented with urea and calcium. In the artificial 259 

sea and reef media supplemented with urea and calcium (ASMCaU and ARMCaU, 260 

respectively), bioconcrete yields from S. pasteurii at the day 16 timepoint were at least 1.5 g/L 261 

higher than those obtained with modified LB medium ( Figure 1A) . Because S. pasteurii 262 

produced more bioconcrete in ASMCaU than in modified lab medium, it could be a promising 263 

bacterium for special-use marine or other high salt applications where the inclusion of urea in 264 

the concrete mix and the release of urease waste products is environmentally acceptable. 265 

Figure 1B shows bioconcrete collected from the S. pasteurii cultures. We observed 266 

changes in the color and coarseness of the bioconcrete grains in the macro scale when different 267 

culture media were used. SEM analysis shows that the bioconcrete was mainly spherical and 268 

agglomerated in morphology, with a range from a few micrometers to ≥20 micrometers ( Figure 269 

2). The EDS results of the samples verified the bioconcrete to be calcium carbonate ( Figure 2 ). 270 

A difference in morphology was observed in the bioconcrete precipitated in ASMCaU and 271 

ARMCaU compared to each other and to the standard lab medium ( Figure 2 , panels C, E, and 272 

A, respectively). Compared to the very smooth, spherical, bioconcrete formed in the modified 273 

lab medium, the bioconcrete formed in the ASMCaU showed much smaller and less rounded 274 

spherical grains that were still very clustered ( Figure 2C ), while the grains precipitated in the 275 

ARMCaU showed more flaky, agglomerated spheres ( Figure 2E ). Of note, the bioconcrete 276 

precipitated in ASMCaU and ARMCaU had small amounts (2.9-4.4%) of magnesium detected 277 

by EDS. The presence of magnesium may impact structurally relevant properties of the 278 

bioconcrete, which should be evaluated in the future. 279

A broad absorption band observed between 1400 and 1500 cm -1 in all S. pasteurii -288

Enrichment culture method to identify bacterial candidates for urea-free marine 304 

bioconcrete applications. Urea supplementation and urease activity for bioconcrete 305 

production increases nitrogen levels, which is detrimental to marine environments (27,28). 306 

Additionally, using bacteria native to the environment where bioconcrete will be implemented 307 

may help ensure that the bacteria are adapted to and will be metabolically active in that 308 

environment. To identify organisms suitable for urea-free marine bioconcrete applications, we 309 

designed an enrichment culture strategy using seawater as inoculum. As depicted in Figure 4 ,310 

to enrich for bioconcrete-forming organisms, we first quantified the bioconcrete produced by 311 

cultures inoculated with seawater. These bioconcrete-producing consortia were cryopreserved 312 

after a 15 day enrichment, and the preserved stock that had produced the most bioconcrete by 313 

weight during the experiment was then struck to isolation. By selecting the highest performing 314

calcium. We compared the bioconcrete precipitation yielded by the three seawater isolates and 338

for 16 days. For the additional strains, we chose Alkalihalophilus pseudofirmus because it is a 340 

commonly studied nonureolytic bioconcrete-forming organism (3,29) that is also alkalophilic, 341

Discussion 387 

In this study, we analyzed bioconcrete production by model and “wild” bioconcrete-388 

forming bacteria in culture media mimicking the sea and reef environments. Our goal was to 389 

identify bacteria that could effectively produce bioconcrete in a urea-independent manner, thus 390 

we designed and employed an enrichment culture-based protocol to identify promising 391 

candidates from seawater. 392 

Our initial step in this work was to reproduce a previously reported method for 393 

bioconcrete quantification (23). That prior work also utilized S. pasteurii as a control, 394 

establishing a baseline by which we could compare bioconcrete yields across their group and 395 

ours. We believe there were several steps that resulted in a loss of bioconcrete or that 396 

introduced variation among replicates in our hands; to address these, we added modifications to 397 

the protocol. First, the amount of bacteria used to inoculate the quantification cultures 398 

significantly influences the bioconcrete yield (33). We thus standardized our inoculation of 399 

bioconcrete cultures to an OD 600nm to 0.001, which likely helped lower the variability we initially 400 

observed across biological replicates. Second, the use of standard glass flasks for culturing 401 

introduces a point of loss, as a substantial quantity of bioconcrete produced during the protocol 402 

adheres to the walls and bottom of the flask and thus cannot be fully quantified. Even thoroughly 403 

mixing the culture at quantification timepoints does not remove the adhered bioconcrete; it 404 

requires scraping off for complete recovery. Thus, we utilized polypropylene 50 mL conicals for 405 

bioconcrete cultures, with an individual conical for each timepoint to be analyzed (i.e., no repeat 406 

sampling from a single culture vessel). Also, we observed that centrifugation for 2 minutes was 407 

insufficient to fully form the bioconcrete pellet, especially for cultures that make finer grains. 408 

Removing supernatant prior to fully forming the pellet also causes a loss in the amount of 409 

bioconcrete able to be quantified, and this loss would differ by the morphology of the 410

bioconcrete itself. Increasing the centrifugation time to 4 minutes helped achieve a more stable 411 

pellet for washing. Notably, centrifuged bioconcrete does not form a settled-enough pellet for 412 

typical decanting. We removed the supernatant instead by vacuum aspiration to prevent losing 413 

some of the bioconcrete pellet during each wash step. Finally, due to the high static charge, the 414 

dried bioconcrete should be resuspended in water then transferred to the vessel that it will be 415 

weighed in before drying. This ensures that all the produced bioconcrete can be quantified. With 416 

these modifications, we were able to greatly reduce the loss of the bioconcrete yield as seen in 417 

Figure S1 where the amount recovered increased from ~3.3 g/L to 6 g/L, the expected yield. At 418 

the same time, we improved the reproducibility of our results by reducing the variation among 419 

biological replicates. This is important because reproducibility is crucial when the quantity 420 

produced is being used as a measure to distinguish various bacterial species in order to identify 421 

top bioconcrete formers. 422 

We used an environmental sample (seawater) as inoculum to identify high bioconcrete-423 

producing consortia, then isolated top performers from the consortia that precipitated the most 424 

bioconcrete, all without including urea. Our approach varies from the recent literature. Many 425 

recent studies focus on isolating bacteria first (generally with urea in the medium), testing them 426 

for ureolytic activity, and, typically, then choosing the most ureolytic isolates to test for 427 

bioconcrete-forming abilities (2,23,34–42). Some deviations from this include Kakad and 428 

Shede’s study in 2023 performing an enrichment culture in media containing calcium before 429 

isolating the bacteria from their samples, and Ekprasert et al. in 2020 isolating non-ureolytic 430 

bacteria by streaking to isolation colonies that made bioconcrete crystals (43,44). Enrichment 431 

cultures that allow early elimination of non- or low-producing bioconcrete formers, such as what 432 

we present here, may allow more expedient isolation of better-performing bacteria from various 433 

environments. Additionally, prior researchers studying bioconcrete production in marine 434 

environments designed an artificial seawater recipe with expensive ingredients (45,46); our 435 

media used here are inexpensive and straightforward to produce. 436 

Due to the higher yield of bioconcrete by the sea isolates B. sp. SBS7 and M. sp. SBS5 437 

in comparison to the other non-ureolytic bacteria studied, these isolates may be more promising 438 

candidates for bioconcrete application in the sea environment than the commonly studied S. 439 

pasteurii and Ahb. pseudofirmus . Due to its ability to sporulate and its higher viable cell count at 440 

the end of the 16 day experiment, B. sp. SBS7 is especially promising, as sporulation will allow 441 

it to withstand the stresses of incorporation into a concrete structure as well as lie dormant until 442 

the structure cracks; also, with more viable cells, there likely can be more healing. As the field of 443

S. pasteurii produces bioconcrete very quickly owing to its high ureolytic activity. 448 

Conversely, the non-ureolytic organisms presented here produced bioconcrete on a relatively 449 

much slower scale - 16 days to the 1 day of S. pasteurii . The speed of production therefore can 450 

impact the application for which each of these bacteria may be suited for. Slower production 451 

may allow for deeper crack healing (inside-out healing) and typically has the benefit of not 452 

requiring urea or producing ammonia. Faster healing may be better for sealing the surface 453 

quickly to prevent the ingress of harmful ions, but at the expense of requiring urea and 454 

potentially poor healing of deeper cracks. For our application of a submerged reef substrate, we 455 

posit that the slower mechanism would prove for better healing and longevity of the reef 456 

substrate, without requiring urea that may negatively impact the reef colonizers. This requires 457 

further investigation. 458 

Our sea isolates form bioconcrete on the timeline of weeks rather than days, and we did 459 

not enrich for ureolytic or denitrification activity while culturing them. Carbonic anhydrases are 460 

nearly ubiquitous in cellular organisms, but vary in quantity and activity across species (50). We 461 

hypothesize that our seawater isolates use their carbonic anhydrase activities to produce 462 

bioconcrete in the culture conditions used here. Browsing their genome annotations in NCBI 463 

shows that all three seawater isolates are predicted to encode at least two carbonic anhydrases, 464 

with SBS7 predicted to encode four. Future work will be required to determine the exact 465 

mechanism(s) underlying the urea-free production of bioconcrete by these isolates. It follows 466 

that with the bioconcrete enrichment protocol detailed here, we may actually be enriching for 467 

isolates with higher carbonic anhydrase activity over others in the consortium. This higher 468 

activity may be due to the presence of more carbonic anhydrase genes/classes of carbonic 469 

anhydrase, higher transcriptional activity, faster enzymes/substrate turnover, or some 470 

combination of these. We propose that further study of these possible factors may enable the 471 

discovery of useful strains for carbon-negative, urea-free bioconcrete production. 472
