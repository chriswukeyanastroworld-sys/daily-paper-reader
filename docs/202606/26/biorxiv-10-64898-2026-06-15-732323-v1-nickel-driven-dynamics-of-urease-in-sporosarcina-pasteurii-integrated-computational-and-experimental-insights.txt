Abstract 9

Urease is a nickel-dependent enzyme that plays an important role in urea hydrolysis and in a 10 

process named as microbial-induced calcium carbonate precipitation (MICP), which is widely 11 

used in sustainable environmental biotechnology. Despite its ecological importance, urease 12 

powers Biogrout (biocementation), a promising green technology for soil stabilization and 13 

infrastructure repair. Yet, the relationship between nickel availability, enzyme activation, and 14 

bacterial fitness remains poorly understood. In this study, we reveal a striking dual effect of 15 

nickel on Sporosarcina pasteurii : while high Ni²⁺ concentrations strongly inhibit growth (IC₅₀ 16 

≈ 637.7 μM), they simultaneously boost specific urease activity up to six-fold. This uncoupling 17 

between biomass and enzymatic efficiency highlights a previously overlooked adaptive 18 

strategy under metal stress. Using structural bioinformatics and molecular docking, we show 19 

that Ure1—the catalytic subunit—exhibits the strongest nickel affinity (−4.3 kcal·mol⁻¹), 20 

supported by highly conserved active-site residues, whereas accessory proteins UreE and UreG 21 

display moderate and weak binding, consistent with their roles in metal delivery and GTP-22 

dependent maturation. In addition, microscopic observations confirmed that calcium carbonate 23 

precipitation was most pronounced at intermediate nickel concentrations (approximately 400-24 

1000 μM), whereas higher concentrations (≥1000-1300 μM) led to reduced mineral formation 25 

due to loss viable cells. Taken together, these results indicates that nickel availability controls 26 

both urease activation and bacterial fitness, and that an optimal balance is required to maximize 27 

biomenerilization efficiency in environmental applications, particularly in biocementation 28 

technology. 29 

Importance 30 

Urease-driven biomineralization is widely used in sustainable technologies such as soil 31 

stabilization and self-healing concrete. However, optimizing these systems requires a clear 32 

understanding of how environmental factors influence enzyme performance. This study shows 33 

that nickel, an essential cofactor for urease, plays a dual role by enhancing enzymatic activity 34 

while inhibiting bacterial growth at high concentrations. By integrating experimental data with 35 

computational analysis, we demonstrate that efficient biomineralization depends on 36 

maintaining nickel within an optimal range that balances enzyme activation and microbial 37 

viability. These findings provide practical guidance for improving biocementation processes 38 

and highlight nickel as a key regulator of urease-based environmental biotechnology 39 

applications. 40

1. Introduction 44 

Urease (urea amidohydrolase, EC 3.5.1.5) is a remarkable nickel-dependent enzyme that 45 

catalyzes the breakdown of urea into ammonia/ammonium and carbonate, a reaction that 46 

highlights nitrogen cycling and microbial survival in diverse ecosystems [1,2]. By raising the 47 

local pH via buffering effect of ammonia/ammonium, urease influences soil fertility and nutrient 48 

availability, making it a key player in agriculture and environmental processes [3]. This enzyme 49 

is found across plants, fungi, and bacteria, serving roles that range from nutrient acquisition to 50 

pathogenicity. In medicine, urease is infamous as a virulence factor in Helicobacter pylori ,51 

enabling the bacterium to colonize the acidic gastric environment [4]. In biotechnology, urease 52 

has gained attention for its role in biocementation—a sustainable approach to soil stabilization 53 

and infrastructure repair through microbially induced calcium carbonate precipitation (MICP) 54 

[5,6]. 55 

At the molecular level, urease operates through a highly specialized catalytic mechanism. Its 56 

active site contains a dinuclear nickel center coordinated by histidine residues and a57 

carbamylated lysine, with a bridging hydroxide acting as the nucleophile [7,8]. This architecture 58 

makes nickel indispensable for enzyme activation. Urease maturation depends on accessory 59 

proteins—UreD, UreF, UreG, and UreE—that manage nickel trafficking and insertion into the 60 

catalytic core [9,10]. When nickel is scarce, urease remains inactive due to incomplete assembly; 61 

when nickel is excessive, it becomes toxic, disrupting metal homeostasis and inducing oxidative 62 

stress [11,12]. This delicate balance between cofactor necessity and toxicity is critical for 63 

microbial survival and for engineering applications. 64 

Despite decades of research, we still lack a clear understanding of how nickel availability 65 

regulates urease activity in environmental bacteria such as Sporosarcina pasteurii , a model 66 

organism for biomineralization [13,14]. Most studies focus on pathogenic systems or controlled 67 

laboratory conditions, leaving unanswered questions about thresholds between nickel deficiency, 68 

enzymatic saturation, and metal-induced stress [15,16]. This knowledge gap limits our ability 69 

to optimize urease-driven processes in real-world applications like biocementation and 70 

wastewater treatment, where both microbial resilience and catalytic efficiency are essential 71 

[17,18]. 72 

To address these gaps, this study investigates how varying Ni²⁺ concentrations influence the 73 

growth and urease activity of S. pasteurii . By combining experimental assays with 74 

computational analyses of urease subunits and accessory proteins, we aim to uncover the 75 

structural and functional determinants of nickel interaction. These insights will not only advance 76 

our understanding of urease biology but also provide practical strategies for engineering robust 77 

ureolytic systems in metal-variable environments. 78

3.1 Effect of different concentrations of Ni 2+ on bacterial growth, activity and specific 201 

activity 202 

Exposure to increase concentrations of Ni²⁺ resulted in a concentration-dependent inhibition of 203 

bacterial growth, as measured by OD 600 (Figure 1). The growth decreased progressively from 204 

the control to the highest tested Ni²⁺ concentration (1100-1300 μM), indicated a clear toxic 205 

effect of nickel on cellular proliferation. 206 

In contrast, urease activity and specific urease activity increased with rising Ni²⁺ concentration, 207 

reaching their highest values at elevated Ni²⁺ levels (Figure 1). This trend was observed despite 208 

the marked reduction in biomass, suggesting differential sensitivity of growth and enzymatic 209 

activity to Ni²⁺ stress. 210 

Despite reduced growth, ammonium concentrations remained relatively stable across treatment, 211 

decreasing only slightly from 0.093 ± 0.06 mM in the control to 0.072 ± 0.001 mM at 1300 212 

μM Ni 2+ . This suggests that ureolytic activity was maintained even under conditions of nickel-213 

induced growth stress (Table 1) 214 

Bacterial growth, measured as optical density at 600 nm (OD 600 ), exhibited a clear 215 

concentration-dependent decrease with increasing Ni²⁺levels (Figure 1). The highest growth 216 

was observed in the control (0 μM Ni²⁺), while a pronounced reduction occurred between 400 217 

and 600 μM. at Ni²⁺concentrations ≥ 1000 μM, bacterial growth was strongly inhibited, 218 

approaching minimal OD 600 values. One-way ANOVA confirmed that Ni²⁺concentration had a 219 

highly significant effect on bacterial growth (p < 0.001) (Table 2). 220 

Total urease activity showed comparatively moderate variation across the tested 221 

Ni²⁺concentrations (Figure 1). While a slight decline in activity was detected at higher Ni²⁺ 222 

levels. The magnitude of change was substantially lower than that observed for bacterial growth. 223 

Statistical analysis indicated a significant effect of Ni²⁺ concentration on urease activity (p < 224 

0.05) (Tale 2) 225 

In contrast to growth inhibition, specific urease activity increased progressively with increasing 226 

Ni²⁺ concentration (Figure 1). The highest specific activity values were recorded at 1000-1300 227 

μM Ni²⁺, despite severe suppression of bacterial growth. One-way ANOVA revealed a highly 228 

significant effect of Ni²⁺ concentration on specific urease activity (p < 0.001) (Table 2). 229

The Cytoscape visualization showed a dense and modular interaction network, with edges 311

operon worked together. The overall topology showed that the urease system in S. pasteurii 313 

operates as a cohesive multi-protein complex, where the catalytic subunits and accessory 314 

proteins interact in a sequential and cooperative manner to achieve enzymatic activation. The 315 

overall topology revealed that the urease system in S. pasteurii functions as a single multi-316 

protein complex, with catalytic subunits and accessory proteins interacting in a sequential and 317 

cooperative way. This organization is necessary because adding nickel and activating enzymes 318

followed by catalytic subunits come together to form an active enzyme. These coordinated 320 

interactions ensure efficiency, prevent misfolding or incorrect metal loading, and ultimately 321 

guarantee the precise regulation of urease activity. 322

4. Discussion: 475 

In S. pasteurii , urease activity and bacterial growth are both significantly influenced by nickel 476 

availability. The current findings demonstrate a dual regulatory function, serving as a necessary 477 

cofactor for urease maturation while also having detrimental effects on cellular development 478 

at high concentrations. The classical heavy-metal stress response in bacteria, where excess 479 

nickel disrupts membrane integrity, displaces essential metal ions, and induces oxidative 480 

damage, is reflected in the concentration-dependent inhibition of growth with an IC 50 of 637.7 481 

μM [12,26,27]. The ureolytic bacteria's adaptability to nickel-rich microenvironments, where 482 

effective nickel handling systems and urease maturation pathways are highly established, is 483 

consistent with the comparatively high tolerance observed here. 484 

In contrast to growth inhibition, specific urease activity increased markedly with rising Ni²⁺ 485 

concentrations and reached its maximum between 900 and 1020 μM. This uncoupling between 486 

biomass production and enzymatic efficiency highlights the absolute dependence of urease on 487 

nickel for catalytic activation. Urease is synthesized in an inactive apo-form and requires 488 

precise insertion of two Ni²⁺ ions into its active site to become functional [7,10]. At low nickel 489 

availability, incomplete metal loading limits enzyme activation, whereas increased Ni²⁺ 490 

concentration promotes full maturation of urease and sharply enhances its catalytic efficiency. 491 

Similar patterns have been reported in other ureolytic organisms, including H. pylori and K. 492 

aerogenes , where nickel supplementation elevates urease activity even when growth is not 493 

significantly stimulated [28,29]. 494 

The observation that total urease activity remains relatively stable across a broad nickel range, 495 

despite declining biomass, further supports the concept of metabolic prioritization of urease 496 

under nickel stress. Fewer cells are present, yet each cell contains a higher proportion of fully 497 

activated enzyme. This behavior suggests that urease is maintained as a high-priority metabolic 498 

function, reflecting its importance in nitrogen metabolism, pH regulation, and environmental 499 

adaptation. Comparable prioritization has been reported for other nickel-dependent enzymes 500 

and metalloprotein systems [15,30]. 501 

The non-linear relationship between ammonium production and nickel concentration also 502 

indicates the existence of an optimal nickel window. Maximum ureolytic output occurred at 503 

intermediate Ni²⁺ levels, while both deficiency and excess resulted in reduced performance. 504 

Such bell-shaped responses are characteristic of metalloenzymes whose activity depends on a 505 

balance between metal availability and toxicity [17,27]. This finding has direct implications 506

for the optimization of urease-based biotechnologies, demonstrating that increasing nickel 507 

concentration beyond a threshold does not translate into enhanced system performance. 508 

The PPI network analysis highlights the highly coordinated nature of urease maturation. Strong 509 

connectivity between the catalytic subunits (UreA, UreB, and UreC) and the accessory proteins 510 

(UreD, UreE, and UreG) reflects the sequential and interdependent pathway required for nickel 511 

delivery and enzyme activation [9,10]. The central role of UreC as a hub protein is consistent 512 

with its structural and catalytic importance and has also been reported in recent network-based 513 

analyses of urease systems [31,32, 33]. 514 

Physicochemical and evolutionary analyses further support this functional hierarchy. Ure1 515 

displayed the highest stability and conservation at its catalytic and metal-binding residues, 516 

underscoring its role as the rigid scaffold that coordinates the dinuclear nickel center [7,8]. 517 

UreE showed properties typical of metallochaperones, including hydrophilicity and 518 

conservation of histidine-rich motifs for transient nickel binding [34]. UreG, a SIMIBI-family 519 

GTPase, exhibited greater instability and conserved P-loop motifs, consistent with its 520 

regulatory role in energy-dependent nickel transfer [21,35]. 521 

Docking results, while semi-quantitative, align well with these functional assignments. Ure1 522 

showed the strongest relative affinity for Ni²⁺, UreE exhibited intermediate affinity suitable for 523 

metal trafficking, and UreG displayed weaker interactions, reflecting its indirect role in nickel 524 

handling. Similar binding hierarchies have been reported in recent computational studies on 525 

urease maturation systems [22,32]. These findings reinforce the concept that nickel regulation 526 

is distributed across a coordinated protein network rather than being confined to the catalytic 527 

enzyme alone. 528 

A particularly important extension of the present work is the microscopic examination of 529 

calcium carbonate precipitation, which provides direct visual evidence linking Ni²⁺-regulated 530 

urease activity to biomineralization efficiency. Under conditions containing urea and calcium, 531 

abundant CaCO₃ nucleation and crystal growth were observed around bacterial cells, 532 

confirming the classical mechanism of microbially induced calcium carbonate precipitation 533 

(MICP). At low to moderate Ni²⁺ concentrations (100–1000 μM), where specific urease activity 534 

was maximal, dense crystal networks were formed, indicating rapid carbonate supersaturation 535 

and efficient mineral nucleation. In contrast, at higher Ni²⁺ levels (≥1000–15000 μM), crystal 536 

formation was markedly reduced or absent, reflecting the combined impact of metal toxicity 537 

and reduced microbial viability, despite the persistence of elevated urease activity on a per-cell 538 

basis. 539 

These findings are fully consistent with the pioneering biocementation work of Al-Thawadi, 540 

who demonstrated that ureolytic bacteria act both as biochemical catalysts and as physical 541 

nucleation templates for CaCO₃ formation, leading to nanoscale calcite particles that 542 

progressively consolidate soil matrices [36,3]. The present study extends this framework by 543 

identifying nickel as a key upstream regulator of this process through its control over urease 544 

maturation and catalytic efficiency. Thus, Ni²⁺ indirectly governs not only enzyme activity but 545 

also the kinetics and morphology of mineral precipitation. 546

to sustain both active enzyme populations and sufficient microbial biomass. 552 

From an applied perspective, these results have direct implications for soil stabilization, self-553 

healing concrete, and subsurface sealing technologies. Maintaining nickel concentrations 554 

within an optimal operational window is essential for maximizing carbonate production while 555 

preserving long-term system stability. This conclusion complements and strengthens the 556 

biocementation strategies originally proposed by Al-Thawadi and co-workers and provides a 557 

quantitative biochemical foundation for refining urease-driven engineering applications 558

Overall, this study demonstrates that nickel is not merely a passive cofactor for urease but a 560 

key regulatory factor of a tightly integrated metalloprotein network that controls enzymatic 561 

activity, microbial fitness, and biomineralization efficiency. The integration of growth kinetics, 562 

enzyme assays, docking, PPI analysis, evolutionary conservation, and microscopic mineral 563 

observations provides a comprehensive framework for understanding and optimizing nickel-564 

dependent urease systems in both environmental and biotechnological contexts. 565

Conclusions 567 

Nickel availability is a decisive factor in controlling urease performance in S. pasteurii , shaping 568 

outcomes at both molecular and physiological levels. Our findings reveal that optimal Ni²⁺ 569 

concentrations enable precise assembly of the dinuclear nickel center, driving peak urease 570 

activity, while excessive levels compromise bacterial viability, with an IC₅₀ of 637.7 μM 571 

marking a critical threshold. These biochemical dynamics translate directly into 572 

biomineralization behavior: when nickel is well-balanced, dense and stable CaCO₃ crystals 573 

form; when toxicity prevails, microbial activity declines and crystal formation deteriorates. 574 

Computational insights further highlight a coordinated hierarchy among urease-related proteins, 575 

underscoring the complexity of nickel handling during enzyme maturation. 576 

Collectively, this work establishes nickel as a central regulator of urease-driven processes and 577 

provides a mechanistic framework for optimizing microbial biomineralization in engineered 578 

environments. For practical applications such as biocementation and soil stabilization, success 579 

depends on striking the right balance—enough nickel to activate urease, but not so much that 580 

it undermines microbial health. 581
